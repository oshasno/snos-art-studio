Learning Philosophy

General Mindset

  • Art doesn’t need to be accurate, it just needs to be convincing.
  • You already have everything you need to learn anything you want. You just need to use your brain.
  • An artist is not limited by medium (digital, traditional, mouse, tablet, software). Why? Because drawing is mostly a brain skill, not muscle memory. Muscle memory mainly helps with line confidence and control, not understanding.
  • Discard perfectionism. There is no point where an artist is 100% satisfied, there will always be more to learn.
  • Improvement is not linear. You will inevitably plateau at times.
  • Get used to things not turning out how you imagined, it’s not the end of the world.
  • After a lot of “bad” drawings, you will eventually create one you’re proud of. If you knew you would make something amazing after failing 100 times, you’d fail much faster.
  • Don’t get too attached to your pieces. Draw and move on.

Practical Tips

Zoom out and don’t get lost in the itty bitty details. Most of it isn't even noticeable.

Learn to simplify (into basic shapes, structure, etc).

Think in 3D, not in 2D. Everything has volume.

One idea applies everywhere, and logic transfers across all things. A cube used for perspective can also define the human body. What you learn from shading fat can be applied to other soft objects. Logic repeats across nature, design, and anatomy; your job is to recognize those patterns and reuse them intelligently.

learning Cycle

Art is like any other skill, and learning it happens as a loop: attempt → fail → correct → retry → improve.

1. Knowledge

Understand the subject deeply. Not mandatory, but it helps. Remember, art doesn’t need to be fully accurate, it just needs to be convincing.

Example: studying muscle groups on an ecorché model or understanding robotic joint mechanics.

2. Observation

AKA breakdowns. Take an image and analyse it by drawing over it to identify structure, perspective, construction etc. Reverse engineer how it was made and understand the logic behind it.

3. Practice

Grab an empty canvas and attempt to recreate it from scratch using your breakdown from the previous step It won’t be perfect, but this is where the learning happens.

4. Feedback

Compare your attempt to the original. Analyse differences in shape, proportion, perspective, colour, anything that you were trying to learn from this. Pinpoint mistakes and carry that feedback into your next attempt.
